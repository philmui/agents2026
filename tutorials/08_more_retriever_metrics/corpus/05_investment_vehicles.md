# Ways to Invest in Precious Metals

There are several ways to gain exposure to precious metals, and they differ in cost, convenience, and the kinds of risk they carry.

**Physical bullion** means bars and coins you actually own. Coins such as widely recognized one-ounce bullion coins are easy to buy and sell but carry a **premium** over the spot price that covers minting and distribution. Bars have lower premiums per ounce, especially in larger sizes. Owning physical metal means you must consider **storage** and **insurance**, and you face no issuer credit risk, only the risk of theft or loss.

**Exchange-traded funds (ETFs)** let you buy a share that tracks the metal's price through a brokerage account. They are convenient and liquid and avoid storage hassles. The distinction between **allocated** and **unallocated** holdings matters: allocated metal is specific bars set aside in your name, while unallocated metal is a claim on a pool and carries **counterparty risk** if the institution fails. Many funds hold allocated bullion in a vault; some products are merely backed by contracts.

**Mining stocks** are shares in companies that produce metal. They offer **leverage** to the metal price, because a small rise in the metal can produce a larger rise in a miner's profit, but they also add company-specific risks: management, debt, mine accidents, and country risk. Mining stocks can fall even when the metal rises if the company stumbles.

**Futures and options** are contracts traded on exchanges such as COMEX. They allow large exposure with a small deposit, called **margin**, which means high leverage and the potential for losses larger than the initial outlay. They are powerful tools for hedging and speculation but are generally unsuitable for beginners.

A simple rule of thumb: physical metal maximizes control and minimizes counterparty risk but costs more to buy and store; funds maximize convenience; miners and futures maximize potential return and risk.
