# What Drives Precious Metal Prices

Precious metal prices respond to a handful of large macroeconomic forces. Understanding them helps explain why prices move even when nothing has changed about the metal itself.

The most important driver for gold is **real interest rates**, the interest rate minus expected inflation. Gold produces no income, so its appeal rises when the **real yield** on safe assets like government bonds falls, and weakens when real yields climb. Much of gold's big moves can be traced to changing expectations about central-bank policy.

The **US dollar** is the second major force. Metals are priced globally in dollars, so a stronger dollar tends to push dollar metal prices down, all else equal, and makes the metal more expensive for buyers using other currencies. A weaker dollar tends to support prices.

**Inflation** matters, but in a subtle way. Metals are often called inflation hedges, and over long periods they have roughly held purchasing power. Over short periods the link is weak, and what really matters is inflation relative to interest rates, which loops back to real rates.

**Geopolitical and financial risk** drives the safe-haven bid. Wars, banking crises, and sharp equity sell-offs can send investors into gold quickly. These moves can be large but are often temporary, fading when the fear passes.

Finally, **supply and demand specifics** matter more for the industrial metals. Silver, platinum, and palladium react to factory activity, vehicle production, and mine disruptions in a way gold largely does not. This is why gold is the purest "macro" metal while the others blend macro forces with an industrial cycle. No single indicator predicts prices reliably; the forces interact and sometimes cancel out.
