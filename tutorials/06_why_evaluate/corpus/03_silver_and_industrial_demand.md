# Silver: Half Money, Half Industrial Metal

Silver behaves like two assets at once. Like gold, it is a **monetary and investment metal**, bought as bars, coins, and funds and treated as a store of value. Unlike gold, a large share of silver demand is **industrial**, because silver is the best electrical and thermal conductor of all metals and is also highly reflective.

Industrial uses include **solar photovoltaic cells**, electronics and electrical contacts, brazing alloys, and medical and antibacterial applications. Solar demand in particular has grown sharply as the world builds more renewable energy capacity. This industrial exposure means silver is partly a bet on the manufacturing economy: when global industry is expanding, silver demand tends to rise.

Because silver is both an investment metal and an industrial input, its price is **more volatile** than gold's. In a strong economy with rising investment interest, silver can outperform gold; in a downturn, weak industrial demand can make it fall faster. Silver's smaller market size, compared with gold, also amplifies price swings.

Analysts watch the **gold-silver ratio**, which is simply the gold price divided by the silver price. It tells you how many ounces of silver it takes to buy one ounce of gold. A historically high ratio is sometimes read as a sign that silver is cheap relative to gold, and a low ratio the reverse, though the ratio can stay stretched for long periods and is not a precise timing tool.

On the supply side, most silver is produced as a **by-product** of mining other metals such as copper, lead, zinc, and gold. This is important: when a silver price falls, by-product supply does not necessarily fall with it, because the mine's main purpose is the other metal. That can keep silver supply higher than its own price would otherwise justify.
