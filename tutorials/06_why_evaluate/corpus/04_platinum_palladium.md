# Platinum and Palladium: The Platinum-Group Metals

Platinum and palladium belong to the **platinum-group metals (PGMs)**. They are rarer than gold and have strong industrial demand, which makes their prices sensitive to both supply disruptions and shifts in technology.

Their single largest use is in **autocatalysts**, the catalytic converters that clean exhaust gases in vehicles. Palladium is used mainly in **gasoline** engines, while platinum has traditionally been used more in **diesel** engines. Because of this, the two metals can be partly substituted for one another, and large price gaps between them push manufacturers to switch, slowly, from the expensive one to the cheaper one.

Supply is highly **concentrated** geographically. A very large share of the world's platinum and palladium comes from a small number of producers, chiefly in **South Africa** and **Russia**. This concentration means that mine strikes, power shortages, or sanctions can cause sharp supply shocks that move prices far more than for a metal with diversified supply.

A major long-term uncertainty is the shift to **battery electric vehicles**, which use no catalytic converter and therefore need no PGMs for emissions control. Faster electrification reduces future autocatalyst demand, especially for palladium. At the same time, **hydrogen fuel cells**, which use platinum, are a potential new source of demand that could partly offset the decline. These offsetting forces make PGM forecasting genuinely difficult.

For investors, platinum and palladium are less liquid than gold and silver and are bought less often as pure safe havens. Their prices behave more like those of an industrial commodity tied to the **automobile cycle** than like a monetary metal, which is why a recession that hurts car sales can hit them hard.
