# Gold: Supply, Demand, and the Safe-Haven Role

Gold is unusual among commodities because very little of it is consumed. Almost all the gold ever mined still exists, mostly as bars, coins, and jewelry. This large **above-ground stock** means gold's price is driven less by yearly mine output and more by shifts in what holders are willing to buy or sell.

On the supply side, the main sources are **mine production** and **recycling** of old jewelry and scrap. Mine supply changes slowly, because new mines take a decade or more to develop. On the demand side there are four broad buckets: **jewelry** (historically the largest, concentrated in India and China), **investment** (bars, coins, and exchange-traded funds), **central banks**, and **technology** (a small slice, used in electronics).

**Central banks** deserve special attention. They hold gold as part of their foreign reserves, and when many central banks buy at once, as happened in several recent years, it provides a strong source of demand that is fairly insensitive to price. Conversely, large official sales can weigh on the market.

Gold's most famous property is its reputation as a **safe-haven asset**. During financial crises, sharp stock-market declines, or geopolitical shocks, investors often move money into gold, pushing its price up while riskier assets fall. Gold is also seen as an **inflation hedge** and a hedge against a weakening US dollar, though these relationships are noisy and hold better over long horizons than over any single month.

A key driver to remember is **real interest rates**, meaning interest rates after subtracting inflation. Gold pays no interest or dividend, so when real rates are high, holding gold has a high **opportunity cost** and its price tends to face pressure. When real rates are low or negative, that cost shrinks and gold often does well. This is why gold frequently reacts strongly to central-bank policy and inflation surprises.
